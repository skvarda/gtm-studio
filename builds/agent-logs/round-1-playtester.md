# Playtesting Report — Round 1

**Build:** Pre-build (design phase)
**Playtester:** Claude | **Date:** 2026-03-04

---

## 1. What Works Well

The core tension — burn rate vs. growth — is immediately legible. Players instinctively understand "money goes down, make it go up." That's a strong foundation. The Harvest Moon structure (day cycles, resource management, relationship-building) maps surprisingly well onto startup life. Hiring feels like planting crops; shipping features feels like harvesting. The mystery co-founder is a genuine hook — players will keep going just to find out what his deal is.

Four divergent endgame paths give real replayability, and hiding some paths initially is smart design.

## 2. Top 3 Anticipated Bugs / Broken Experiences

**BUG-001: The First 10 Minutes Problem.** If the player starts with just a laptop and a burn rate timer, there's no clear first action. Without a strong tutorial beat or obvious first move (e.g., "your first customer emails you"), players will stare at the screen. **Severity: Critical.**

**BUG-002: Developer vs. Salesperson — False Choice.** If one hire is strictly dominant early (likely: developer, since you need a product to sell), this isn't a real decision. It'll feel solved on first playthrough. **Severity: High.**

**BUG-003: Co-Founder as Dead Weight.** If the mystery NPC just stands in the office doing nothing with no interaction hooks for 2+ hours, players will forget he exists. The payoff won't land. He needs small, weird micro-interactions from day one — cryptic Post-it notes, unexplained whiteboard diagrams, packages arriving. **Severity: Medium.**

## 3. Feel Assessment

On paper, yes — this feels like a game. But the risk is it becomes a spreadsheet. The 16-bit aesthetic must do heavy lifting: satisfying animations for shipping code, a jingle when you close a deal, visual office growth. Without juice, it's a dashboard.

## 4. Pacing

Unclear. The day-cycle length will make or break this. Too short and decisions feel rushed; too long and the early game drags before you can hire. Recommend: short days (60–90 seconds real-time), first hire available by day 3.

## 5. Single Highest-Impact Improvement

**Nail the first hire decision.** Make developer-vs-salesperson a genuine fork with visible, immediate consequences. The salesperson should be able to land a contract *before* the product exists (promising vaporware — very startup-authentic). The developer should unlock a prototype that attracts inbound interest. Both viable, both risky, both funny. This one decision will tell you if the game's strategic core actually works.
