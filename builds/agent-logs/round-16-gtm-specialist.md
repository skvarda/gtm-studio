# GTM & Narrative Design Doc — Round 16

## 1. The Core Viral Hook

**"What's your number?"** The victory screen is built for screenshots. A clean stats card — victory path name, days survived, peak cash, total hires — styled like a startup pitch deck slide. Players will naturally compare: "Day 22 IPO" flex vs. "Day 34 Profit Machine" grind respect. The four distinct paths create tribal identity. IPO chasers argue with Profit Machine purists. World Domination players call everyone else casuals. This is organic replay pressure without us asking for it.

## 2. Key Narrative Beats

- **Day-end victory check** replaces the quiet fade-to-black with a moment of earned weight. The screen tints. Three lines of dialog. No fanfare music — just a beat of silence, then the stats card.
- **Save/load continuity** means players invest across sessions. "Continue (Day 14)" on the title screen is a quiet promise: your grind matters.
- **Game-over at $0 cash** now hits harder because victory was *possible*. Near-misses create the "one more run" compulsion.

## 3. Co-Founder Evolution

After Day 15: a second monitor appears on his desk. No dialog. No explanation. Players who notice will wonder. Players who don't will catch it on a replay. After any victory triggers, he stands — the only time he moves in the entire game — and delivers one line. Then fade. The restraint *is* the hook. Forum threads write themselves: "What is the co-founder building?"

## 4. Signature Dialog

**IPO Victory, Line 2:**
*"The board wants you to ring a bell. You don't own a bell. You ring the Slack notification sound instead."*

## 5. Startup Tropes & GTM Mechanics

- **Profit Machine as the "quiet skill" path** mirrors real bootstrapper culture — less flashy, deeply respected by those who know.
- **World Domination requiring aggressive sales hiring** teaches the actual GTM lesson: market domination is a headcount problem.
- The stats card mimics a Series A deck slide intentionally. Players internalize startup metrics (MRR, burn, customer count) through play. That's stealth literacy — and it makes the game resonate with exactly the audience most likely to share it.
