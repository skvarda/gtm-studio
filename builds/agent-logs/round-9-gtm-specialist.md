# GTM & Narrative Design Doc — Round 9: "The First Hire"

## 1. Core Viral Hook

**"Who did you hire first?"** This is the debate that splits every founder community. Dev-first vs. sales-first is a religious war in startup Twitter. We lean into it. The game should produce shareable moments: screenshots of Day 12 bankruptcy because you hired two devs and no one to sell, or a sales-heavy team pitching vaporware. The hiring decision is our "which starter did you pick?" moment — it defines your run identity and is inherently debatable. Future share cards: *"I went double-dev and IPO'd on Day 45. Sales is a myth."*

## 2. Key Narrative Beats

- **The Door Opens**: First time interacting with the door post-tutorial, the tone shifts from "you're alone in here" to "you could bring someone in." This is the game's first real strategic branch.
- **The Office Gets Louder**: Each hire adds a line to the end-of-day summary. The solo grind becomes a team narrative. Wins feel earned collectively; failures sting more with mouths to feed.
- **Burn Anxiety Escalates**: The cash counter drops faster visibly. Player should *feel* the runway shortening. Tension is the mechanic.

## 3. Co-Founder Evolution (Subtle)

The co-founder says nothing about your hires. That's the point. Everyone else generates end-of-day results — "Dev Priya shipped +5%", "Sales Marcus closed $90 MRR." The co-founder's line? Absent. He produces no visible output. The whiteboard roster doesn't list him. If the player interacts with him after hiring, he says: *"Good. More people."* No elaboration. The mystery deepens through omission, not exposition.

## 4. Signature Dialog

> *You post a job listing on Hacker News. 47 comments debate your tech stack. 3 people mass-reply "Will you sponsor my visa?" One person applies. She's overqualified and wants equity, free LaCroix, and a standing desk you don't own. You hire her immediately.*

## 5. Startup Tropes to Incorporate

- **Burn rate dread**: Cash counter turns amber at 14 days runway, red at 7
- **The "we're hiring!" cope**: Door interaction text mirrors real job posts — aspirational nonsense masking desperation
- **Role ambiguity**: Salespeople occasionally generate bug reports; devs occasionally suggest features no one asked for (flavor text in end-of-day)
- **Office capacity as landlord excuse**: "Your lease only covers 6 desks" — the cap feels diegetic, not mechanical
