# GTM & Narrative Design Doc — Round 10

## 1. Core Viral Hook

**"My startup died on Day 3."** The game's viral loop is the shareable failure story. Players screenshot their burn rate hitting zero, post their ridiculous hiring decisions, and compare how many days they survived. The early game is deliberately tight on cash — most first runs end in bankruptcy within a week. That friction creates retry compulsion and social sharing. The 16-bit aesthetic makes every death screen screenshot-ready.

## 2. Key Narrative Beats This Round

- **Morning Dread**: Each day opens with a one-liner flavor text. "The coffee machine is broken. Morale is theoretical." These set tone and make the day cycle feel alive, not mechanical.
- **The First Hire**: The single narrative moment we're shipping. Choosing your first team member is irreversible and framed as momentous — a tiny pixel person walks through the office door, sits down, and says something that hints at their personality. Developer: pragmatic. Salesperson: overconfident.
- **The Quiet Office**: Before hiring anyone, the office feels empty. After the hire, ambient details shift. This environmental storytelling rewards attention.

## 3. Co-Founder Evolution

He's at his desk. He's always at his desk. This round, one small addition: if the player walks past him, a thought bubble appears with a single `...` and disappears. No dialogue, no interaction. Players will wonder if it's a bug. It isn't. Seed planted.

## 4. Signature Dialogue

When hiring the first salesperson:

> **CHAD**: "I closed a $400K deal at my last company. Well, *assisted*. Well, I was in the Slack channel. Anyway — when do I start?"

## 5. Startup Tropes & GTM Mechanics

- **Burn rate anxiety** as core tension — real founders feel this immediately
- **Build vs. sell dilemma** mirrors the actual Series A death trap: ship product or chase revenue?
- **Hire #1 as identity choice** — developer-first signals "product company," salesperson-first signals "revenue company." This maps to real GTM strategy and creates divergent playthroughs
- Daily revenue ticks teach unit economics intuitively: each customer = recurring cash, each employee = recurring cost
